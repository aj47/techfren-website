# There Is No Permanent Underclass

This week, I kept seeing the same fear in different forms:

- Are we entering a permanent underclass?
- Is AI about to split society into permanent winners and permanent losers?
- Is the window to adapt already closing?

I get why people feel this way. The pace is insane. The capability jumps are real. Jobs are already being reshaped.

But I still don’t buy the permanent underclass outcome.

My position is simple:

> A permanent underclass requires a permanent monopoly.

And history, economics, and the way AI tools are spreading all suggest that kind of monopoly is extremely hard to maintain.

## Respect the fear, reject the conclusion

Some of the strongest voices warning about this are not unserious people. They’re seeing true signals:

- AI is compressing timelines.
- Knowledge work is being automated faster than expected.
- The cost of producing software, media, and services is collapsing.

That part is true.

Where I disagree is the final leap: “therefore most people are permanently locked out.”

Massive disruption is likely.
Permanent lockout is not.

## The core economics: markets need two sides

Here’s the logic that keeps getting skipped.

A functioning market needs producers **and** buyers.

If a small elite used AI to eliminate most people’s ability to earn, they’d also eliminate demand for what they’re selling. That isn’t a stable economy. That’s collapse.

A monopoly with no customers is not a durable empire. It’s a dead end.

So even from a purely self-interested perspective, concentration has limits. Systems that kill broad participation eventually undermine themselves.

## The hidden assumption: the pie is fixed

Most permanent-underclass takes assume a fixed pie:

- there’s one pile of value,
- AI lets incumbents seize all of it,
- everyone else can only fight over scraps.

That model misses how technology waves actually work.

The pie doesn’t stay fixed. New categories appear. New demand appears. New interfaces create new businesses that were previously impossible.

Ten years ago, many of today’s creator and software-native jobs barely existed.
The next decade will create roles we can’t name yet.

## AI lowers barriers faster than institutions can close them

AI is not only concentration technology. It’s also distribution technology.

It reduces the cost of:

- building products,
- acquiring skills,
- testing ideas,
- operating globally with tiny teams.

The same tools used by large companies are increasingly available to small teams and individuals. That matters.

When barriers drop, edge opportunities appear everywhere:

- niche products,
- vertical services,
- personal software,
- creator-led businesses,
- solo operators with leverage that used to require entire orgs.

This does not mean everyone wins automatically.
It means opportunity surface area expands.

## Human steering still matters

I work close to these tools every day. I’m not arguing from distance.

AI is getting better at execution, but high-value outcomes still depend on:

- taste,
- problem selection,
- trust,
- distribution,
- real-world context,
- persistence under uncertainty.

In other words: steering.

The most important question isn’t “Will AI replace all human effort?”
It’s “Who learns to direct this new leverage toward useful outcomes?”

## My practical conclusion

I’m not saying this transition will be painless. It won’t.

I am saying the binary story—“permanent overclass vs permanent underclass decided right now”—is too simplistic and economically weak.

A better framing:

- Disruption is real.
- The transition is urgent.
- Monopoly outcomes are unstable.
- Competition creates openings.
- New markets emerge.
- Agency still matters.

We should focus less on predicting a frozen class system and more on helping more people become builders in this new environment.

That’s how you turn AI from fear into abundance.
